Strategic Thinking

Chess Principles
Applied to Grappling

Chess and grappling share the same decision architecture. The tactics that win at the highest levels of chess describe exactly what happens in the best grappling chains.

The Fork
Chess: Attack two pieces at once

Attack two submission threats simultaneously. The armbar-triangle chain is the classic fork: your armbar defence is my triangle setup. Your opponent cannot cover both. This is why chains beat isolated attacks — always be setting up the next attack while the first is live.

The Pin
Chess: Lock a piece in place

In grappling, a pin is positional control that forces your opponent to carry your weight or concede position. Side control, mount, knee-on-belly — you aren't just stopping their movement, you're forcing them to spend energy holding a bad position. Pins score no points but open submissions.

Remove the Defender
Chess: Take the piece protecting the king

Every escape has a defending limb. The cross-face removes the underhook. The whizzer removes the back-take. Identify what is defending the position you want — and remove it before attacking the position itself. Don't attack the king when the knight is still guarding it.

Control the Center
Chess: Dominate the board from the middle

In grappling, the center is your opponent's hips and spine. Whoever controls the hips controls the match. This is why hip escapes are the most important defensive movement in BJJ — and why shutting down your opponent's hip mobility is the first priority from any top position.

Overworked
Chess: Force a piece to defend two threats

The arm that's defending the choke can't also defend the armbar. The hand that's gripping your collar can't also stop the sweep. Chain your attacks to create overloaded defenders. This is the Danaher principle: create a problem in one area to generate a solution somewhere else.

Remove Escape Routes
Chess: Cut off the king's flight squares

Before finishing a submission, block every exit. For the rear naked choke: second hook in before the arm threads. For heel hook: perpendicular hip angle before the cup. For the triangle: lock the figure-four before pulling the head. An escape route left open is a submission lost.

Preparatory Moves
Chess: Set up the combination first

The grip fight before the throw. The posture break before the submission. The underhook battle before the back take. Most successful techniques are invisible — they were set up three moves earlier. Identify what has to be true for your technique to land, and create those conditions first.

A Man with a Plan
Chess: Know your next three moves

If defended, go to Y. If Y is defended, go to Z. Every attack should have a pre-decided secondary outcome so a stuffed attempt never resets to neutral. Armbar → triangle → back take. Guillotine → armbar → triangle. Plan your chain before you start it.

Technical Detail

Grip Taxonomy

Not all grips are equal. The grip you choose determines how much force you can transmit, how quickly you lose it, and what techniques it enables. From Galibert's mechanical analysis.

Gable Grip
Clinch control, bodylock, pummelling

Palms facing each other, fingers interlaced. Highest overall strength. Go-to for clinch control, bodylock passes, and any grip where you need maximum structural integrity.

S-Grip
Armbar isolation, kimura setup

Each hand hooks the other in an S-shape. Allows rotation. Standard for armbar isolation from mount (pull the arm across, S-grip to prevent the clasp). Weaker than gable under direct pull.

Figure-Four / Kimura Grip
Kimura, omoplata, guillotine variants

One hand grips the wrist; the other arm threads over and grabs its own wrist. Creates a locked triangle of force that can transmit enormous shoulder pressure with minimal grip strength.

Three-Finger Grip
Gi only — collar and lapel control

Middle three fingers inserted deep in the collar. Maximises choking pressure for collar chokes. Less structural than the gable — not for pulling, only for applying rotational choking force.

Ball and Socket
Rear clinch, body triangle, seatbelt

One arm hooks over the shoulder, the other under the armpit — hands meet at the sternum. The body's most stable rear control position. The platform for every back attack and the seatbelt grip.

RNC Grip
Rear naked choke, bow-and-arrow entry

Choking arm under the chin, bicep seals one carotid, free hand cups the back of the head. Pull elbows together and arch — do not just squeeze. Squeezing the bicep alone rarely finishes at competition level.

Heel Cup
Inside/outside heel hook, ashi-garami

Palm against the back of the heel (inside), or palm against outside of heel (outside). The cup direction determines the rotation direction. No substitute exists — a heel hook without the cup is a straight ankle lock.

Idiot Grip
What not to do

Thumbs wrapped inside the fingers. Named because it risks finger breaks when the opponent yanks free. Never use the idiot grip in live rolling — hook your fingers, keep the thumb on the outside.

Range Management

Fighting Distances

Every technique has a range. Managing distance — entering and exiting the right range at the right time — is as important as the technique itself.

Out of RangeNeither athlete can attack. Use this range for observation: stance, weight distribution, movement patterns. Where is their weight? Are they circling? This tells you which entries will be available when you close distance.
Kicking RangeSingle strikes. For grapplers: the range you pass through quickly. Don't linger here — you have no takedown grip, no clinch, and you're fully exposed to strikes. Enter or exit, don't idle.
Clinch RangeGrip fighting, collar ties, underhook battles. Your first grappling range — where wrestling shots and judo throws live. Everything starts here. Establish your grip before committing to an entry.
Takedown RangePenetration step distance. Close enough to shoot or throw. Kuzushi happens here — break the balance before the technique. The fastest path from neutral to back control runs through this range.
Grappling RangeGround work, guard passing, submissions. The arena where BJJ dominates. Whoever manages the transition from clinch to ground on their terms controls what happens next.
